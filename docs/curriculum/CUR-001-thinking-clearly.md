# 1. Module Title

Thinking Clearly

---

# 2. Purpose

Thinking Clearly helps learners slow down thought, examine claims, and avoid
confusing feelings or assumptions with facts.

The module does not promise perfect objectivity or freedom from error. It gives
the learner a repeatable way to notice what they observed, what they felt, what
they assumed, how they interpreted it, and what still needs examination.

The aim is not to make the learner hesitate about everything. The aim is to
make careful thought more available before a reaction becomes a conclusion.

---

# 3. Skills Trained

This module trains the learner to practice:

- separating observation from interpretation;
- identifying assumptions;
- recognizing uncertainty;
- forming clearer questions;
- examining evidence;
- avoiding rushed conclusions.

These skills support one another, but each lesson introduces one primary
distinction at a time.

---

# 4. Lessons

## Lesson 1 — What happened vs. what it means

### Concept

Separate an observable event from the meaning assigned to it.

### Short explanation

An observation describes what could have been seen, heard, counted, or
recorded. An interpretation explains what the learner thinks the observation
means.

Interpretations are often necessary, but they are not identical to the event.
Keeping them separate makes it easier to examine whether another explanation
also fits.

### Example

> My friend looked at their phone twice while I was speaking. They do not
> respect me.

What happened: the friend looked at their phone twice.

What it might mean: the learner interprets this as disrespect.

Other explanations may exist. The observation alone does not establish which
explanation is true.

### Exercise

Rewrite each statement as two lines: **what happened** and **what I think it
means**.

1. “My colleague did not answer my message today, so they are avoiding me.”
2. “I made two mistakes in the presentation, so the whole presentation was a
   failure.”
3. “My parent sighed when I spoke, which means they are disappointed in me.”

Do not decide whether the interpretation is correct yet. Practice separating
it from the observation first.

### Reflection prompt

Which words in your original statement turned the event into a meaning?

### Steward practice prompt

> Help me separate the observation from my interpretation: my friend looked at
> their phone while I was speaking, so I think they do not respect me.

## Lesson 2 — Feelings are real, but not always final evidence

### Concept

Treat a feeling as real experience without treating it as final proof of an
external claim.

### Short explanation

A feeling is real because the learner is experiencing it. Fear, shame, anger,
relief, and confidence can carry important information about needs,
expectations, or previous experience.

A feeling does not automatically prove what another person intends, what will
happen, or what the learner is worth. Clear thinking neither suppresses the
feeling nor asks it to settle every factual question.

### Example

> I feel certain that I will fail the interview.

The anxiety is real. The future outcome remains uncertain. The feeling may
show that the interview matters or that the learner expects difficulty, but it
does not provide the interview result in advance.

### Exercise

Choose one strong feeling and complete these statements:

- I feel…
- The feeling may be responding to…
- The feeling does not yet prove…
- One fact I can check is…

Keep the feeling in the account. Do not dismiss it or turn it into a verdict.

### Reflection prompt

What changes when you respect the feeling without asking it to prove the whole
story?

### Steward practice prompt

> I feel sure I will fail, even though it has not happened. Help me respect the
> feeling while separating it from what is known.

## Lesson 3 — Assumptions and missing information

### Concept

Identify what a conclusion requires the learner to assume.

### Short explanation

An assumption fills a gap between available information and a conclusion.
Some assumptions are reasonable. Others are habits, fears, or guesses that
have not been checked.

Naming an assumption does not prove it false. It shows which part of the
learner's reasoning depends on information that may still be missing.

### Example

> They have not replied, so they must be angry with me.

Known: no reply has arrived.

Assumed: the person saw the message, had an opportunity to reply, chose not to
reply, and did so because they were angry.

Missing information: whether the message was seen, what else is happening,
and what the person actually thinks.

### Exercise

Take this conclusion:

> My manager scheduled a meeting, so I am going to lose my job.

List:

1. what is known;
2. every assumption needed to reach the conclusion;
3. what information is missing;
4. one way to obtain better information.

### Reflection prompt

Which assumption felt like a fact before you wrote it down?

### Steward practice prompt

> My manager scheduled a meeting and I am assuming I will lose my job. Help me
> identify the assumptions and missing information without giving me false
> reassurance.

## Lesson 4 — Better questions create better thinking

### Concept

Replace a loaded or unanswerable question with a question that can be
examined.

### Short explanation

Questions direct attention. A question containing an absolute claim, assumed
motive, or impossible demand for certainty can make clear thinking harder.

A better question does not guarantee a comfortable answer. It identifies what
the learner actually needs to understand and makes relevant evidence easier to
find.

### Example

> Why does nobody ever listen to me?

This question assumes “nobody” and “ever” are already established.

A clearer question might be:

> What happened in the last conversation where I felt unheard, and what did I
> need the other person to understand?

The clearer question creates something specific to examine.

### Exercise

Rewrite each question so that it is specific and examinable:

1. “Why does everything go wrong for me?”
2. “How can I know this decision is definitely right?”
3. “Why are they trying to make me look foolish?”

Remove unsupported certainty without removing the concern behind the
question.

### Reflection prompt

Did your revised question change the answer you were looking for, or only make
the real question clearer?

### Steward practice prompt

> Help me turn this into a clearer question without dismissing it: why does
> nobody ever listen to me?

## Lesson 5 — Evidence, alternatives, and consequences

### Concept

Examine a claim by comparing evidence, alternative explanations, and likely
consequences.

### Short explanation

Clear thinking does not mean collecting only evidence that supports the first
conclusion. It asks what supports the claim, what weakens it, what alternatives
fit the facts, and what follows if the claim is acted upon.

Alternatives do not need to be equally likely. Their purpose is to test whether
the first explanation has become certain too quickly.

### Example

> If I raise the problem, the relationship will become worse.

Evidence may include previous reactions or the seriousness of the problem.
Alternatives may include careful communication improving understanding,
changing nothing, or revealing a conflict that already exists.

Consequences differ depending on timing, wording, safety, and the other
person's response. The claim needs examination rather than automatic belief or
rejection.

### Exercise

Choose one claim you are considering and create four short sections:

- evidence that supports it;
- evidence that weakens it;
- two alternative explanations;
- one likely consequence of acting as though it is true.

Then identify what new evidence would most improve your judgment.

### Reflection prompt

Which mattered more to your conclusion: the amount of evidence or the quality
of the evidence?

### Steward practice prompt

> I think speaking up will make everything worse. Help me examine the evidence,
> alternatives, and consequences without deciding for me.

## Lesson 6 — From reaction to examination

### Concept

Create a short pause between an initial reaction and a chosen conclusion or
action.

### Short explanation

Reactions can be fast and useful. They can alert the learner to danger, hurt,
conflict, or importance. They can also combine a fact, feeling, assumption,
and conclusion before the learner notices the steps.

Examination does not require suppressing the reaction. It slows the sequence
enough to separate its parts and choose the next question responsibly.

### Example

> That message was rude. I should send an angry reply now.

Possible separation:

- fact: the message contained specific words;
- feeling: anger or hurt;
- interpretation: the sender intended disrespect;
- assumption: an immediate forceful reply will improve the situation;
- next question: what response addresses the words clearly without acting on
  an unexamined motive?

### Exercise

Use one recent reaction and write five lines:

1. Fact:
2. Feeling:
3. Assumption:
4. Interpretation:
5. Next question:

Wait until all five lines are present before choosing whether to act.

### Reflection prompt

Which part of the reaction became easier to examine once it had its own line?

### Steward practice prompt

> I received a message that felt rude and want to reply immediately. Help me
> separate the fact, feeling, assumption, interpretation, and next question.

# 5. Practice Conversations

The learner may use these prompts with Steward after completing the
corresponding lessons:

1. > I was left out of a meeting, and I think this proves my team does not
   > value me. Help me separate what happened from what I think it means.
2. > I feel ashamed after making a mistake. Help me understand what the feeling
   > tells me without treating it as proof of my worth.
3. > Someone has not replied to me, and I am assuming they are upset. Help me
   > identify what I know, what I assume, and what is missing.
4. > My question is “Why do I always ruin everything?” Help me form a clearer
   > question that I can actually examine.
5. > I think changing my plan will disappoint everyone. Help me examine the
   > evidence, alternatives, and consequences.
6. > I reacted strongly to a comment. Help me separate the fact, feeling,
   > assumption, interpretation, and next question before I respond.

These are practice invitations, not tests of agreement. The learner may
revise, reject, or stop any line of examination.

---

# 6. Completion Criteria

The module is complete when the learner can take one confusing thought and
separate:

- **fact** — what was directly observed or is otherwise established;
- **feeling** — the learner's real internal experience;
- **assumption** — information being supplied without confirmation;
- **interpretation** — the meaning assigned to the available information;
- **next question** — one focused question that would improve examination.

The learner should be able to explain the distinction in their own words and
apply it to at least one unfamiliar example.

Completion records demonstrated practice. It is not a grade of intelligence,
character, judgment, or worth, and it does not claim that the learner will
never rush to a conclusion again.

---

# 7. Non-goals

This module does not teach:

- debate tactics;
- manipulation;
- emotional suppression;
- certainty where certainty is unavailable.

It does not train the learner to distrust every feeling, avoid decisions,
defeat another person in argument, or postpone action indefinitely.

Thinking clearly is not the same as feeling nothing. It is the practice of
knowing which part of a thought is fact, feeling, assumption, interpretation,
or an open question.

# 8. Constitutional Traceability

This module derives its learning boundaries from the following sources:

- **LS-004 — The Limits of Life School:** Life School does not claim final
  understanding or replace human judgment. This supports intellectual honesty,
  humility, and acknowledgment of uncertainty.
- **LS-007 — Proposals and Contributions:** ideas are examined on their merits,
  contributions remain open to correction, and significant claims enter an
  examination process. This supports truthful examination rather than
  authority by identity or position.
- **CS-003 — Uncertainty:** learners distinguish facts from assumptions,
  identify what is known and unknown, and avoid fabricated certainty.
- **CS-008 — Learning:** Steward supports understanding and skill development
  without replacing the learner's thinking.
- **CS-009 — Curiosity:** learners explore questions, alternatives, and
  perspectives while preserving intellectual honesty and humility.

Terminology note: the current Foundation documents are canonically titled
LS-004 “The Limits of Life School” and LS-007 “Proposals and Contributions.”
This module uses **intellectual honesty** and **truthful examination** as
derived curriculum concepts; it does not rename those constitutional
documents.

The module preserves learner judgment throughout. It supplies distinctions,
exercises, and questions, but it does not determine which interpretation or
conclusion the learner must accept.
