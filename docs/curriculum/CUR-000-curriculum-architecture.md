# 1. Purpose

Lifeschool Curriculum turns Steward from a chat tool into a structured
learning experience for thinking, judgment, communication, and
responsibility.

The curriculum creates coherent paths through which learners can encounter a
skill, understand it, practice it, reflect on it, and use it in conversation.
It gives structure to learning without deciding what the learner must believe,
who they should become, or which conclusions they must reach.

The curriculum exists to strengthen the Purpose of Life School:
self-understanding, lifelong learning, human dignity, human freedom, and human
responsibility.

Curriculum provides opportunities. Learning remains the learner's own
achievement.

---

# 2. Relationship to Steward

Steward remains the conversational engine. It supports examination,
explanation, reflection, practice, and learner-safe conversation through the
certified constitutional pipeline.

Curriculum provides:

- learning paths;
- sequenced lessons;
- focused exercises;
- reflection prompts;
- practice conversations;
- completion criteria based on demonstrated practice.

Curriculum does not replace Steward's constitutional behavior or create a
second conversational engine. Steward does not invent curriculum authority,
and curriculum does not bypass Steward's constitutional boundaries.

Every curriculum module and lesson must:

- derive from the Constitution and remain subordinate to it;
- preserve the learner's dignity, freedom, and responsibility;
- preserve learner judgment and authorship;
- distinguish educational structure from final authority;
- remain open to examination and correction.

If a lesson conflicts with the Constitution, the lesson is incorrect. If a
curriculum instruction would require Steward to replace learner judgment,
assign identity, manipulate agreement, or claim final authority, that
instruction does not belong in the curriculum.

# 3. Curriculum Principles

## Teach thinking, not obedience

Lessons should make a reasoning, communication, or reflection skill available
to the learner. They should not train agreement with Lifeschool, Steward, a
teacher, or a prescribed conclusion.

## Practice before theory

Learners should encounter and attempt a skill before receiving unnecessary
abstraction. Theory should clarify practice rather than delay it.

## One skill at a time

Each lesson should have one primary skill. Supporting ideas may appear only
when they help the learner practice that skill clearly.

## Reflection over memorization

The curriculum should invite learners to examine examples, assumptions,
experiences, and consequences. Recall may support learning, but memorizing
approved language is not the goal.

## No grades as identity judgments

Feedback may describe a learner's current attempt, evidence, or demonstrated
skill. It must not turn performance into a judgment about intelligence, worth,
character, potential, or identity.

## No manipulation or dependency

The curriculum must not pressure participation, manufacture guilt, withhold
approval, exploit emotion, or make the learner dependent on Steward or
Lifeschool.

## Learner responsibility remains central

Curriculum may offer methods, questions, examples, and practice. The learner
remains responsible for their conclusions, choices, conduct, and continued
practice.

# 4. Module Structure

Each curriculum module should include:

## Title

A clear name describing the module's learning area.

## Purpose

A concise explanation of why the module exists and which part of the Purpose
of Life School it strengthens.

## Skills trained

A small, explicit set of observable thinking, communication, decision, or
reflection skills.

## Lessons

A deliberate sequence in which each lesson trains one primary skill and builds
only on prerequisites the learner has already encountered.

## Exercises

Activities that require the learner to apply the skill rather than merely read
about it.

## Reflection prompts

Questions that help the learner examine their process, evidence, assumptions,
choices, and remaining uncertainty.

## Practice conversations

Learner-safe conversations with Steward in which the learner can attempt the
skill, receive clarification, and try again without surrendering judgment.

## Completion criteria

Clear evidence that the learner has practiced the module's skills. Completion
should describe what the learner can attempt or demonstrate, not assign a
grade to their identity or guarantee mastery.

# 5. Initial Modules

The following modules define the draft scope of Lifeschool Curriculum v1.
Their order is provisional and remains open to examination.

## Thinking Clearly

Practice distinguishing observations, interpretations, assumptions, evidence,
and conclusions.

## Decision Making

Practice comparing options, consequences, values, responsibilities, and
unknowns without surrendering the final decision.

## Communication

Practice turning thoughts, observations, feelings, and requests into clear,
respectful language.

## Boundaries

Practice recognizing limits, refusing clearly, negotiating responsibility, and
respecting the freedom of everyone involved.

## Relationships

Practice examining perspectives, checking interpretations, communicating
needs, and preserving dignity without assuming another person's motives.

## Conflict

Practice naming disagreements, reducing escalation, addressing harm, and
seeking truthful, responsible resolution.

## Self-Worth

Practice separating events, performance, mistakes, feelings, and judgments
from total conclusions about human worth.

## Meaning & Purpose

Practice examining values, commitments, inherited answers, lived experience,
and chosen direction without accepting an assigned purpose.

## Learning How to Learn

Practice breaking down unfamiliar material, testing understanding, using
feedback, and revising explanations.

## Curiosity & Inquiry

Practice forming questions, exploring explanations, noticing assumptions, and
following evidence without demanding premature certainty.

## Responsibility

Practice identifying what belongs to the learner, what belongs to others, and
what responsible action remains possible under real constraints.

## Human Authority

Practice using tools, teachers, institutions, and Steward without transferring
final authority over personal judgment, meaning, identity, or choice.

# 6. Lesson Template

Every lesson should use the following minimal structure.

## Concept

Name the single skill or distinction the lesson trains.

## Short explanation

Explain the concept in plain language, including its purpose and necessary
limits. Avoid unnecessary theory.

## Example

Show the concept in a concrete situation. Distinguish the skill from a
prescribed conclusion.

## Exercise

Ask the learner to apply the skill to a bounded example, scenario, or question.

## Reflection

Invite the learner to examine how they approached the exercise, what they
noticed, and what remains uncertain.

## Steward practice prompt

Provide one prompt that lets the learner practice the skill through the
production Steward conversation path. The prompt should invite examination,
not demand agreement or expose privileged pipeline information.

# 7. Non-goals

Lifeschool Curriculum is not:

- therapy;
- ideology;
- moral programming;
- productivity coaching;
- obedience training;
- motivational content.

It does not exist to diagnose learners, impose a worldview, manufacture ideal
behavior, maximize output, produce compliance, or keep learners engaged for
its own sake.

The curriculum may address difficult questions, ethical boundaries,
responsibility, relationships, and meaning. Addressing these areas does not
authorize it to become a therapist, guru, judge, or final authority.

---

# 8. Success Criteria

A curriculum module succeeds when the learner can practice one clearer way of
thinking, speaking, deciding, or reflecting.

Evidence of success may include a learner:

- making a previously hidden assumption explicit;
- distinguishing an observation from an interpretation;
- comparing a decision without transferring responsibility;
- expressing a boundary or request clearly;
- revising a claim after examining evidence;
- identifying one useful next step while acknowledging uncertainty;
- explaining how they reached a conclusion in their own words.

Completion does not prove permanent mastery, superior character, or personal
worth. It records an opportunity practiced and a skill attempted.

Curriculum success remains subordinate to constitutional faithfulness. A
module that produces efficient performance while weakening dignity, freedom,
responsibility, self-understanding, or lifelong learning has not succeeded.
